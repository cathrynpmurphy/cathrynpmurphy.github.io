Thermodynamics and Reliability in Stochastic Circuits

Physical bits are subject to thermal noise, which can trigger unintented bit flip errors. In CMOS technology, errors are typically suppressed by increasing the voltage bias across its transistors. However, miniaturization drives circuits into regimes where the voltage cannot be simplt increased and where engineering must account for thermal noise. The physical origins of this thermal noise is the conduction of individual, discrete electrons, which are stochastically triggered by the state of the circuit. Capturing the thermodynamics of the circuit requires that we build up the dynamics of the circuit from these discrete microscopic events. Such a task quickly becomes computationally intractable with existing methods. We deploy tensor networks algorithms to build up and solve the dynamics and thermodynamics while preserving the microscopic origins of stochastic noise.

We use tensor networks and a basis expansion to tractably compute the first two eigenvectors with DMRG. The first eigenvector, which has a zero eigenvalue, represents the steady state, which we can use to compute the circuit dissipation from local detailed balance relationships. The first non-zero eigenvalue captures to the slowest dynamical timescale, allowing us to characterize the bit-flip error time.

With these tools, we quantify the effect of connectivity on the reliability of CMOS bits – a physical tradeoff which was previously intractable with existing methods – and show that long-range correlations between connected units augment memory reliability.

Cathryn Murphy, Schuyler Nicholson, Nahuel Freitas, Emanuele Penocchio, Todd Gingrich. arXiv:2603.04658 [cond-mat.stat-mech]
